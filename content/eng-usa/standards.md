# Lesson 1: Standards-Based Approach to Translation Quality

This lesson focuses on a standard of good practices by [ASTM F43](https://www.astm.org/membership-participation/technical-committees/committee-f43), a technical committee that produces standards on language services and products. That standard is [ASTM F2575-25 Standard Practice for Language Translation](https://store.astm.org/f2575-25.html). Standards by ASTM F43 inform the entire approach outlined in these course materials. Here, we'll focus upon two important sections from F2575: the sections on **specifications** and **translation labels**.

## Specifications

According to ASTM International, specifications are an "explicit set of requirements to be satisfied by a material, product, system or service" ([Form and style for ASTM standards](https://store.astm.org/media/wysiwyg/FormStyle_4-25-23.pdf)). Within translation and localization (T&L) specifications are particularly important. This is due to the nature of language work, where many different translations and localizations could be correct, and different project stakeholders—including the people who will use the product, requesters, and producers—will have different ideas about what constitutes quality. If you ask a group of five people to review the same content, they’ll all have different ideas and preferences related to quality.

With specifications, the primary objective is therefore to take this fuzzy idea of quality and make it objective. At the end of a project, if we’ve met the requirements in the specifications, we have achieved quality, so long as our specifications are accurate and complete.

Defining accurate and complete specifications is no small matter. Specifications need to address the needs and desires of the requesters of the language services, their clients, and the teams producing the product. Specifications also need to make any implicit expectations explicit. 

That's why using specifications is so important in achieving desired outcomes in writing, translation, and localization. In fact, it's so important that using specifications is the sole recommendation within F2575 that producers wishing to become certified to the standard **must** meet.

## Translation Labels

The F2575 standard was recently updated to include **translation labels** that give requesters, producers, and the people who ultimately use language products a common language for understanding the level of quality (i.e. risk) associated with translated content. Here, an **Unverified Translation** is one that has not undergone professional verification. A **Professionally Verified Translation** has been reviewed by a qualified professional, which tells buyers that they can have confidence in the translation.

It merits emphasis that the distinction made here isn't between translations produced by machines and translations produced by humans. A translation produced by someone who is bilingual who doesn't have the necessary qualifications to do translation is equally risky as raw automatic translation. This model acknowledges that translations are produced through hyprid workflows in which qualified professionals use machine translation as part of their tool belt. The distinction is that a qualified professional has taken responsibility for the final translation.

In producing specifications, one of parameters that needs to be defined is whether Un-Verified Translation will suffice (in cases where nothing more than the gist of the message needs to be understood), and where it's necessary to have Professionally Verified Translation. From there, the people, processes, and technologies used to produce the translation can be defined.

You can read more about what went into the design of these labels [here](https://locessentials.com/blog/2026/01/16/making-an-informed-choice-an-introduction-to-astm-translation-quality-labels/).

| Un-Verified Translation | Professionally Verified Translation |
| ----- | ----- |
| • Raw machine translation<br>• Unchecked LLM-generated translation<br>• Translations by bilingual nonprofessionals | • Bespoke translation by hand<br>• Professionally post-edited automatic translation<br>• Professionally edited nonprofessional translation |

## Standards-Based Structured Translation Specifications

Let's take a look at the [Standards-Based Structured Translation Specifications](https://www.tranquality.info/specs/) by Tranquality that are based on ASTM F2575 to understand the some of the types of parameters that are useful to define when doing translation.

### Source-Content Information

| Parameter | Discussion |
|-------|-------|
| Source language and variant | For example: U.S. English, Mexican Spanish. In technological systems, language variants are typically expressed in two-letter language and country codes, though a shift toward three-letter codes will become necessary as more languages go digital. (There aren't enough two letter codes to go around.) While systems often codify assumptions about which languages are spoken where, the reality of the world thanks to immigration is that country specific variants of languages spoken by diaspora communities should be taken into account. For example, there is a distinct variant of Vietnamese that is spoken in the United States. |
| Subject field | Situating language in a subject field is how meaning is derived by speakers of that language. A rather simplistic example is that of a "mouse". In IT and biology, two distinct concepts are denoted by "mouse." |
| Text type | This parameter defines the shape and linguistic conventions of the content. For example, academic articles and blog posts have distinct presentations and writing contrainsts. |
| Audience | An unforunate reality of T&L is that the audience is often defined no further than be denoting the language variant in which the content will be written. But a country is not an audience. A language is not an audience. Audience is the specific group of people the content is being written for, and helps to further define the specific linguistic conventions to be used. For example, if my audience is "programming students," I know that I'll need to define programming concepts and methodologies in a way that facilitates learning. |
| Purpose | Another parameter for defining linguistic conventions. Purpose includes categories like informational, persuasive, philosophical, and entertainment. Often a piece of writing has multiple purposes. |

Additional parameters covered in the Tranquality specifications in this category: volume, complexity, and text origin.

#### Key Resources

### Target-Content Information

Of course, key parameters to define here are the **target language and variant**, **audience** and **purpose** of the text, especially accounting for shifts in audience and purpose that are the result of systemic-level differences in how societies and organizations are organized between two cultures. 

| Parameter | Discussion |
|-------|-------|
| Correspondence | A good way to understand correspondence is through Lawrence Venuti's framework of domestication and foreignization. A domesticated translation is one in which content corresponds to the expectations of the target audience. A foreignized translation maintains what the target audience would consider to be foreign elements to give them the experience of the source culture. |
| Elements of style | Its a missed opportunity if style guidelines only address mechanical aspects of language usage, such as date formats and measurement conversions. More effective style guidelines address requirements around the voice and personality to be evoked by a translation. |
| Reference materials | Any reference materials that should be followed during translation should be provided. These might include corpora, glossaries, and translation memories (TMs). Where TMs of uncertain origins are provided, quality evaluation to determine if the TM is usable should be performed. |

Additional parameters covered in the Tranquality specifications in this category: **file format** and **layout**.

## Workflow Information 

As discussed in the chapter "[Translation and Localization Project and Process Management](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/9783110716047-008/html)," T&L workflows are divided into pre-production, production, and post-production phases, with strategically placed quality control steps to prevent downstream issues

| Parameter | Discussion |
|-------|-------|
| Pre-production | Pre-production steps include pre-editing the source content for translation and linguistic asset management, such as the corpus building and glossary and style guide creation. |
| Production | Recommended production workflows for translation include translation, editing, and proofreding. |
| Post-production | The post-production phrase includes the incorporation of lessons learned into materials that will be used in subsequent projects. |

## Environment Information

| Parameter | Discussion |
|-------|-------|
| Talent | Talent with the necessary qualifications need to be assigned to projects. Rates and credit for work done also need to be established. |
| Technology | In defining technology usage, both translation technology and the native systems in which content will be used need to be taken into consideration. For many types of content, following the segmented sentence-by-sentence approach codified into translation technology is not recommended, as this produces literal translations that are unlikely to resonate. Whether automatic translation will be used should be defined here too. |
| Communication | Points of contact for addressing questions and making recommendations need to be established, as does the frequency and platforms used for check-ins and status updates. |

In this category, it is also important to define **milestones**, **delivery methods**, and **restrictions** in how the content can be used by producers after the completion of the work.

## Localization Specifications

In these course contents we define translation and localization in keeping with the [framework](https://www.tranquality.info/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/2023-02-20-FIT-TLT-V3g5-withSignedLangEtc_eng-Dec2021.pdf) developed by the International Federation of Translators. That is, we define translation as work with the textual content and localization as the adaptations that are made to a product's design.

It's an unfortunate reality that much of the work that is done in the language industry is not in fact localization. Instead, systems designed for source content users are simply translated, and often badly (i.e. literally).

If localization is to be performed, additional parameters not discussed here need to be established that are related to design and in-context testing and quality reviews.
