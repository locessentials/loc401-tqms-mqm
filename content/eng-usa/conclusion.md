# Conclusion and Lessons Learned

Translation quality is highly subjective. No two translators will produce the same target rendering of the same source passage, and without a shared definition of quality, no two evaluators will grade the same text identically either. Left to their own devices, evaluators succumb to human nature: when assigned an evaluation task, they tend to search out every perceived error, including minor stylistic preferences that aren't errors at all. The more problems they find, the better they feel they're doing their job. This "intuitive" nature of translation quality is precisely what standards by ASTM F43 seek to address.

According to standards, translation quality is the ability to meet the expectations defined in explicit specifications. Only once specifications are in place can quality managers determine the criteria evaluators will use to judge target texts. And even then, both evaluators and translators need training on what each error type actually means in practice because labels like "mistranslation" may be over-applied when a different error label is more appropriate.

So what do fish have to do with any of this? When introducing a new fish to a tank, its ability to breathe water doesn't guarantee its survival if you dump it in directly with the other fish. The shock of the new water temperature can kill it. Even if particularly hardy fish may be able to survive the change in water temperature, that stress is no way to start their life in their new environment. The healthy approach is acclimation: letting the temperatures in the fish's temporary bag and the tank slowly equalize before the fish is released. Only then can it be added to the tank with stress levels kept at healthy levels.

The same logic applies to translators and quality evaluators alike. Quality managers who expect a new translator to understand their organization's standards after a single test—or no test at all in rush circumstances—set that translator up for frustration at best and failure at worst. And evaluators who are handed a typology without calibration training will produce inconsistent results that introduce noise rather than clarity into the production environment. The right approach in both cases is to define quality expectations in explicit specifications, invest in structured onboarding, and recognize that errors specific to your organization shouldn't be held against people who haven't yet been trained on them. Over time, with consistent feedback, both translators and evaluators acclimate. They internalize your definition of quality until the system as a whole, the people, the processes, and the data they generate, is working in alignment.

This course has been about building the systems that make acclimation possible for translators, for evaluators, and for the production environments they work within. Here's what you covered along the way:

- In the **Introduction**, you reflected on what translation and localization quality means to you personally, and were introduced to the MQM framework as a structured vocabulary for describing not just that something went wrong, but what, where, and how much it matters.
- In **Lesson 1**, you learned how ASTM F2575-25 requirements transform subjective quality expectations into measurable outcomes through specifications, and how translation labels distinguish professionally verified work from unverified output.
- In **Lesson 2**, you worked with a customized MQM error typology, nineteen error types across six categories plus the quality marker, and learned how holistic correspondence and readability ratings determine a translation's overall effectiveness.
- In **Lesson 3**, you saw how a TQMS trains evaluation teams to reach consistent results through a scaffolded sequence of projects, and how that consistency is what makes annotation data deployable in production environments.
- In **Lesson 4**, you compared traditional CAT-based workflows against professional annotation environments, and learned to use Label Studio for structured, exportable MQM-based error annotation.
- In **Lesson 5**, you used exploratory data analysis to interpret annotation data, including span overlap, error distribution, and agreement scores, and you learned how to use those findings in calibration sessions.
- In **Lesson 6**, you applied root cause analysis methods to identify the upstream causes of recurring translation errors, and learned how corrective action compounds over time into a more efficient production workflow.

Quality doesn't happen at the end of a project. It's built into every decision made before translation begins and into every system put in place to evaluate, learn from, and improve the work over time. The goal of TQMS is not a single score. It's putting in place a vocabulary, a process, and a feedback loop sturdy enough to keep systems improving iteratively.
