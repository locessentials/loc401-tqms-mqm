# Course Outline

## Introduction: Translation Quality Management Systems Based on MQM

This introduction frames translation quality as a systemic challenge rather than an intuitive judgment, and presents MQM as a structured vocabulary for describing what went wrong, where, and how much it matters. It outlines the input-process-output chain that underlies translation quality management and introduces the plan-do-check-act cycle that runs throughout the course. Learners begin by articulating their own personal definition of translation and localization quality, which they will return to at the end of the course.

## Lesson 1: Standards-Based Approach to Translation Quality

This lesson introduces ASTM F2575-25 and its two key concepts: specifications and translation labels. Specifications transform subjective quality expectations into objective, measurable requirements, while translation labels distinguish Unverified from Professionally Verified translations based on whether a qualified professional has taken responsibility for the final product. The lesson uses the Tranquality structured specifications framework to walk through the parameters worth defining before beginning a translation project.

## Lesson 2: MQM Error Typology & Holistic Quality

This lesson presents a customized MQM error typology with seventeen error types across six categories, along with guidance on how to apply them. It expands on the standard MQM framework by introducing quality markers to recognize particularly well-handled translation challenges, and levels of impact to replace traditional severity levels. The lesson concludes with a holistic rating system that scores translations on correspondence and readability to determine overall effectiveness.

## Lesson 3: Translation Quality Management Workflows

This lesson introduces a Translation Quality Management System (TQMS) for training evaluator teams to assess translation quality consistently. It addresses the central challenge of inter-rater reliability and walks through a scaffolded sequence of training and evaluation projects designed to bring teams into alignment. Once evaluators reach the desired agreement threshold, their annotations can be used to train new evaluators, provide translator feedback, and fine-tune LLM-based quality evaluation systems.

## Lesson 4: Error Annotation Environment

This lesson compares traditional translation environment tools (TEnTs) with professional annotation platforms, examining the structural limitations of sentence-level CAT workflows for quality evaluation. It introduces Label Studio as an open-source annotation environment suited to MQM-based error annotation, and walks through how to configure and use it for structured, exportable quality evaluation.

## Lesson 5: Exploratory Data Analysis

This lesson introduces exploratory data analysis as the primary tool for making sense of annotation data produced by a team of evaluators. It walks through the tools provided in the Translation Quality Evaluation repository, including a Jupyter notebook and an HTML report, covering span overlap visualization, annotator summaries, error type distribution, holistic ratings, and agreement calculations. Learners can apply these tools to their own annotations and use the findings to diagnose where agreement is breaking down and why.

## Lesson 6: Root Cause Analysis and Continual Improvement

This lesson introduces root cause analysis as a method for identifying the upstream causes of recurring translation errors across production environments. It presents two practical RCA methods—Fishbone Diagrams and the 5 Whys—and situates them within a translation workflow using the MQM Root Cause Error List. Learners apply these methods to a real non-conformity and produce a corrective action plan designed to prevent recurrence.

## Conclusion and Lessons Learned

This conclusion uses a fish acclimation metaphor to reflect on what it takes to bring translators, evaluators, and production environments into alignment around a shared definition of quality. It summarizes the key takeaways from each lesson and invites learners to return to the personal quality poster they created in the introduction, reflecting on how their definition has or hasn't changed. Sharing that reflection with colleagues, and sitting with both the similarities and the differences in how people define quality, is how understanding continues to deepen beyond the course.
