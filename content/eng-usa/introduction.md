# Introduction: Translation Quality Management Systems Based on MQM

Welcome to this course on **Translation Quality Management Systems (TQMS)** grounded in the Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM) framework. This course is designed for translation and localization professionals, language quality managers, and machine learning engineers who want to build or refine systematic, standards-based approaches to evaluating translation quality.

**Translation Quality Management** provides an overview of how to systematically manage and evaluate translation quality throughout the production process.

The presentation frames translation as an **input-process-output chain**: the source text and reference materials serve as inputs, skilled human translators or machine translation systems carry out the process, and the resulting translation is then evaluated as output. Quality management, however, encompasses the entire workflow, not just the final check.

Drawing on **ISO 9001**, the presentation outlines five components of a quality management system: quality planning, control, evaluation, assurance, and improvement. These map onto a continuous **plan-do-check-act** cycle, which should be applied at every stage of the workflow.

Key topics covered include the **TEP process** (translation, editing, and proofreading), with an emphasis on avoiding preferential "happy-to-glad" changes and tying accuracy assessments to project specifications. The presentation also distinguishes between **holistic quality** (correspondence to the source and readability as a whole) and **analytic quality** (identifying individual errors using frameworks like the Multidimensional Quality Metrics, or MQM).

Additional concepts addressed include MQM **error severity levels** (neutral, minor, major, and critical) and the importance of **intra- and inter-rater reliability** to ensure consistent evaluations across time and evaluators. The presentation emphasizes that quality management is especially critical in the current era of generative AI and machine translation.

## A Note on MQM

The **Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM)** framework is the foundation for the TQMS presented in this course. It provides a hierarchical error typology that enables consistent, reproducible quality evaluation across language pairs, content types, and production environments.

> MQM is not a single score — it is a structured vocabulary for describing what went wrong, where, and how severely.

This distinction is fundamental to everything that follows. A single numeric quality score tells you *that* something is wrong. MQM tells you *what*, *where*, and *how much it matters*.

## What You Will Learn in this Course

Over eight lessons, you will develop a working knowledge of:

- The standards landscape governing translation quality, including **ASTM F2575**
- The **MQM error typology** and how it maps to holistic quality scoring
- Practical **quality management workflows** used in professional LSP environments
- How to set up and use an **error annotation environment**
- Applying **exploratory data analysis (EDA)** to translation quality metrics data
- Conducting **root cause analysis (RCA)** and implementing preventative corrective action

The error annotations you produce throughout this course have applications beyond quality control: they can train evaluators to apply MQM more consistently, serve as fine-tuning data for LLMs performing automated quality evaluation, and generate targeted feedback to help translators improve. Higher-quality human translations, in turn, can serve as training data to bring LLMs to the same standard.

## Active Learning

We hope that one of the main takeaways that you have after completing this course is that the idea of quality is subjective. While we may agree on some characteristics of what makes quality translated and localized products, each person defines quality in their own way, and each of our perspectives is valid. When teams work together, it becomes necessary for people to harmonize their ideas of quality and make sure that systems are designed to take everyone's needs into account.

## Active Learning

Before diving into the official definitions and frameworks covered in this course, take a moment to reflect on what translation and localization quality means to *you*.

Create a **personal quality poster**, that is, a single visual that captures your definition of translation and localization quality. Your poster should:

- Express your definition using **key phrases of no more than about 10 words each**
- Include **at least one visual element** (an image, diagram, icon set, or other illustration) that represents your thinking
- Be accompanied by a **~150-word description** explaining your choices and what your definition reflects about your background and experience

Your poster doesn't need to be polished. It just needs to be honest. Once you've made it, consider sharing it with teammates, mentors, or industry peers. Use it as a conversation starter: Where do your definitions overlap? Where do they differ? Can your group arrive at a harmonized working definition of quality, or does the exercise reveal that harmonization is harder than it sounds?

That tension is worth sitting with. In the lessons ahead, you'll encounter frameworks like MQM that are designed precisely to bridge individual perspectives and create shared evaluation standards. Coming into those lessons with your own definition already articulated will give you a concrete point of comparison.
